Analytical Solution: Cantilever Beam

The governing partial differential equation for the free transverse vibration of a uniform Euler-Bernoulli beam is:

$$EI \frac{\partial^4 w}{\partial x^4} + \rho A \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial t^2} = 0$$

Assuming harmonic motion, the spatial wave equation is driven by the parameter \(\beta\):

$$\beta^4 = \frac{\rho A \omega^2}{EI}$$

Boundary Conditions (Fixed-Free)

For a cantilever beam:

  1. Fixed End (\(x = 0\)): Displacement and slope are zero (\(W = 0, W' = 0\)).
  2. Free End (\(x = L\)): Bending moment and shear force are zero (\(W'' = 0, W''' = 0\)).

Applying these yields the transcendental frequency equation:

$$\cos(\beta L) \cosh(\beta L) = -1$$

The roots \(\beta_n L\) must be solved numerically. The first few are approximately 1.875, 4.694, and 7.855. For higher modes (\(n \ge 3\)), they approximate to \(\frac{(2n-1)\pi}{2}\).

The exact natural frequencies are:

$$\omega_n = (\beta_n L)^2 \sqrt{\frac{EI}{\rho A L^4}}$$

And the corresponding mode shapes are:

$$W_n(x) = (\cosh \beta_n x - \cos \beta_n x) - \sigma_n (\sinh \beta_n x - \sin \beta_n x)$$

where \(\sigma_n = \frac{\cosh \beta_n L + \cos \beta_n L}{\sinh \beta_n L + \sin \beta_n L}\).

FEM Formulation for Cantilever Beam

A beam element uses cubic Hermite shape functions with two degrees of freedom per node: lateral displacement (\(w\)) and slope (\(\theta\)). The local element matrices are identical to the simply supported beam.

Applying Fixed-Free Boundary Conditions

At the fixed wall (Node 0), both degrees of freedom are fully constrained:

$$w_0 = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \theta_0 = 0$$

All subsequent nodes (Node 1 through Node \(N\)), including the free end, are completely unrestrained. This leaves \(2N\) active degrees of freedom in the global assembled matrices.

Solving the System

The natural frequencies are found by solving the generalized eigenvalue problem:

$$(\mathbf{K}_{\text{active}} - \omega^2\mathbf{M}_{\text{active}})\mathbf{U}_{\text{active}} = 0$$

The system is transformed to a standard symmetric eigenvalue problem using a Cholesky decomposition of the mass matrix, and solved via the Jacobi method.

Mode Shape Between Nodes

The deflection inside each element is not a straight line between the two nodal deflections. It is the cubic Hermite interpolation of both the nodal deflections and nodal rotations:

$$w(\xi) = N_1 w_i + N_2(h\theta_i) + N_3 w_{i+1} + N_4(h\theta_{i+1})$$

where \(\xi = x/h\) is the local coordinate within the element and \(N_1, \ldots, N_4\) are the standard Hermite cubic shape functions. This is what the mode-shape plot above draws for the FEM curve.
